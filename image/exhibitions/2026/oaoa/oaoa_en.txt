Micro-Disaster

OAOA presents Micro-Disaster, a two-person exhibition by Yang Ha and Yoori, as its first exhibition of 2026. The exhibition takes as its subject the point at which social and personal tragedies converge within an individual, and the time that follows, naming this condition a “micro-disaster.”

Disaster is something we all encounter. From war, accidents, and climate catastrophes to the missing-person alerts that regularly sound on our phones, disasters are everywhere, delivered into the palms of our hands at great speed. Even when we are shaken by news of devastation, there are often few things we can do beyond refreshing the page. The guilt and helplessness that arise from this inability to act have become familiar emotional conditions of contemporary life. Long after social systems have been restored and public memory has faded, the pain left behind by disaster does not disappear; it remains, borne by someone in particular.

In personal life as well, death, illness, and unforeseen events arrive without warning and unsettle the ordinary. Though such experiences may be common, for the individual passing through them they often constitute the greatest catastrophe imaginable. Socially, we have not learned enough about how to receive and process such pain, and at times others hastily determine when mourning and recovery should end. In the end, enduring confusion and grief once again becomes something one must carry alone.

Through their respective visual languages, Yang Ha and Yoori focus on these disasters that ultimately become one person’s burden, speaking about life after tragedy. Rather than submitting to socially prescribed rhythms or conventions, they propose ways of confronting catastrophe on one’s own terms and continuing to live.

Yang Ha has long questioned contemporary modes of consuming disaster, referring to the condition in which images of explosions and debris are smoothed and refined through screens as “mediated violence.” The artist transfers scenes of tragedy, collected almost like screenshots, onto canvas, yet the actual weight and pain of those events have been removed. Explosions transformed into pastel clouds or soft, jelly-like forms coolly expose the numbness with which contemporary viewers safely process and pass by catastrophe. These “cute explosives,” casually overlaid onto interiors and views beyond windows, reveal how lightly we register—and how quickly we forget—the suffering of others while remaining comfortably within our daily routines.

In this exhibition, Yang Ha presents a new phase in which the afterimages of explosions that have recurred throughout previous works are transformed into still lifes or portraits. The sleek explosions of earlier paintings become protagonists endowed with bodies, standing confidently at center stage. By placing disaster within a theatrical setting and allowing it to meet the viewer’s gaze directly, the artist strips away external contexts and creates a state of confrontation in which only the object and oneself remain. Responding to the question, “Why do you paint stories from far away?” Yang Ha answers, “I am painting what is closest.” The works suggest that distant tragedies have already passed through our doors and windows, acquiring tangible form and arriving beside the vase on our dining table. Through the titles as well, the artist asks where “close by” truly is, proposing the modest courage that even “0.1% affection” might be enough to sincerely bring another’s suffering into one’s own sphere. Refusing to leave pain as something that belongs only to others, this gesture of incorporating it into one’s own narrative becomes both a dignified resistance against helplessness and a profoundly warm form of solidarity.

Yoori translates interior states—ambiguous emotions and boundaries that resist complete definition through language—into visual forms. Taking as her point of departure the inevitable gaps that arise between words and images, between the two-dimensional and the three-dimensional, she has explored these spaces through painting and artist books. In this exhibition, she invokes the fictional persona of the Kalendermacher (“calendar maker”) to propose diverse understandings of time. The act of making calendars originated in an intensely private discipline: drawing eight vertical lines and five or six horizontal lines as a way of calming anxiety. Faced with disasters too vast to control, imposing temporal structure within a fixed grid became a strange yet singular source of stability. Having become a skilled maker of time, Yoori now dissolves the boundaries between past and future, holding fleeting moments together on a single plane and reconstructing temporal contexts. The textures of layered fabric and the traces of thread visible across the canvas reveal the artist’s careful labor of stitching together moments that scatter and disappear, making the nature of passing time visible and confronting us with aspects of temporality we often choose to ignore.

These explorations extend into three-dimensional form through the materiality of resin and candles. In <A Year - Accumulation Through One Another>, drawings and fragments of fabric appear suspended between layers of resin, granting physical thickness to time and preserving its flow like geological strata. The overlapping forms and colors become faint yet tender traces left behind by a passing year, much like photographs or diary entries. For beings destined to disappear, perhaps what ultimately remains are only such small accumulations of memory and record. Meanwhile, <30 Days - Provisional Forms>, thirty candle-calendars arranged upon stepped pedestals, become an intuitive portrait of time passing through the present. Burning flames and melting wax evoke the irreversibility of time. In their resistance to prediction or prevention, time and disaster resemble one another. Rather than being consumed by this overwhelming force, Yoori fragments the flow of time and imposes order upon it. This quiet yet active gesture—an effort to live through today despite fear and helplessness—offers a resilient source of energy for life after tragedy.

After the tragedies of both distant and intimate worlds have passed, each person is left with something to bear. Throughout the exhibition, Yang Ha’s insistence on looking directly at the moment when disaster reaches the table of everyday life resonates with Yoori’s determination to patiently mend the ruptures left by shattered time and rebuild the strata of living. Refusing to calibrate grief according to socially sanctioned distances or acceptable durations, the two artists offer records of living after catastrophe in their own ways. It is our hope that these records become a small source of courage for each visitor, and an invitation to add just a little more—perhaps only 0.1% affection—to the world around us.
