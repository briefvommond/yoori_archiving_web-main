<Time and the Other>




One day, in the brief moment between closing your eyes and opening them again, you suddenly become someone I can never fully know—a stranger. It is like repeating a word over and over until it begins to sound unfamiliar. We may have faced one another, exchanged things, breathed together, touched one another. We may have felt as though we knew, shaped, and held each other in our hands.

And then, suddenly, we realize that from beginning to end, you and I have existed only as separate beings, enclosed within the boundaries of our respective selves. I do not know you. Before you, the Other, there always stands a demonstrative pronoun that points to a distance that can never be closed. I do not know this Other. I merely mistake the image reflected back to me again and again for an understanding of this or that Other. Therefore, understanding itself cannot be truth. Your image is always there, carrying my misunderstandings along with it. In that sense, understanding is, paradoxically, a complete misunderstanding. The “you” I believed to be closest is, in fact, the farthest away.

Time and the Other, a two-person exhibition by Yoori and Ilkwon Yoon, speaks of the alterity of “you” revealed through intimacy. Throughout the exhibition space, Yoori and Ilkwon Yoon’s works are stacked layer upon layer, adhering to one another and forming large walls without clear boundaries. Their surfaces appear flat, almost like a single plane. They seem harmonized, unified, as though becoming a single body. Yet the accumulated thickness contains within it the space behind the surface, the distance between sheet and sheet. Yoori and Ilkwon Yoon continue to cover one another while remaining themselves.¹ What Yoori paints is overlaid by what Ilkwon Yoon paints; what Ilkwon Yoon paints is overlaid by Yoori’s marks. Here, overlapping and blending are not the same. Within these layered spaces, they never merge, but remain forever on different strata.

¹ The 400 × 800 cm wall installation consists of four interwoven layers made by the two artists, topped by an additional layer formed through their shared choices. Yoori created two layers by connecting fourteen paintings measuring 200 × 120 cm each, while Ilkwon Yoon assembled two layers from twenty-eight works measuring 100 × 120 cm. Another wall piece in the exhibition (400 × 120 cm) was constructed according to the same structure of intersecting layers.

Yet something tears. Within that immense intimacy that is also a distance—a space—an event occurs. For the one who endlessly examines the tedious fact of their own existence and digs furrows called “I,” an encounter with “you” takes place. A crack opens. They are torn together, or damaged separately. On the wall, in order to reveal either “I” or “you,” they have no choice but to tear themselves and one another. Without wounding and rupturing each other, the self cannot exist. Without tearing oneself, one cannot face the other.

Throughout the exhibition, the act of tearing is foregrounded. Ordinarily, “to tear” suggests destruction—ripping apart, mangling, scattering into pieces—and thus easily appears as violence. Yet here, tearing is instead a form of dialogue, a way of facing one another. Yoori and Ilkwon Yoon have spoken of damaging the image while contemplating how to reveal each other. Within that process dwell hesitation, individual accents, respect, consideration, conversation, laughter and sighs, sorrow and joy. All of these are ways of being in relation to one another.

Yoori is someone who grieves in advance for all the sorrows to come—or perhaps for something that cannot even be named sorrow. Her time is filled with unnamed emotions. She has consistently been interested in confronting that which cannot be articulated and then regenerating it once again into another realm beyond language. In this exhibition, her paintings possess far greater spontaneity than before, intensifying these sensations. Yet once the paintings are torn within the exhibition space, this nonverbal realm undergoes another process of de-verbalization enacted through the gesture of tearing. Stripped of their original integrity, the images enter an even more ambiguous state.

This ambiguity and spontaneity stand in marked contrast to Ilkwon Yoon’s medium of printmaking—planned, or rather necessarily planned—and to his way of remembering fading memories through images based on concrete photographs. Ilkwon Yoon’s time unfolds through love for memories and relationships, while accepting their disappearance and preserving their existence through remembrance. His works, too, are torn, emphasizing a process of vanishing. In this way, the two remain subtly out of alignment, overlapping while remaining different.

Yet what they share is this: neither denies nor rejects the alterity of the other. Ilkwon Yoon, within this relationship, “does not choose a method that refuses disappearance,”² but accepts his own dissolution while remaining himself, becoming obscured and torn. Yoori, within the fractures that arise in confronting the other, explores “the processes through which meaning is generated and transformed between different layers, those of the self and the other.”³ Thus, the immense and intimate distance that can never be overcome does not appear to them as the painful negativity of separation. Yoori and Ilkwon Yoon affirm one another as Others existing on separate layers. Their strata, and their times, are different. Yet through that affirmation, another axis of time emerges within the cracks between you and me.

That is what fragments are. The act of tearing ultimately produces fragments. These remnants may form new shapes, be discarded, or be attached again to mend the torn wall. The debris falling from the cracks generates new energies and possibilities within the exhibition space. These fragments are reconfigured. They do not simply lose meaning and fall away; they become entirely new pieces. They are born. They are generated. They are not time trapped within the furrows of the self, but a new time made possible through encounters with the Other.

Through Time and the Other, we are invited to reinterpret the number two and the relationality it implies through alterity, the fractures that accompany it, and the new temporal axes that emerge from them. Intimacy always wears the face of what is most unfamiliar. Understanding wears the face of misunderstanding. The furrows of the self and its fundamental emptiness will always remain, inescapably. There is no avoiding this.

And yet events continue to occur there. Things are broken again. From those cracks, forces are generated—the strength to tear oneself open and to look toward the Other. And at that moment, you are no longer this Other.

You become you.
