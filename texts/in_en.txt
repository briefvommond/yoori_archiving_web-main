《Bind Loosely & Loosen Tightly》 


Mohee (Independent Curator)

<The Age of Silence and Pensées of the Night>

Nicole Krauss once wrote that in the age of silence, people did not communicate less; rather, they communicated more. The earliest language of that time was gesture, and there was no distinction between the gestures of language and the gestures of life. “Building a house or preparing food, for example, was a form of expression no less meaningful than a gesture that conveyed ‘I love you’ or ‘I mean it.’”¹ It was an era in which every expression carried its own unique weight. Longing for a time I have never known, I find myself measuring the weight of today’s words and writing. Language that has left our bodies and lingers in the air. When we momentarily step away from it—when we grasp what cannot be conveyed through speech and text—something finally becomes perceptible. Rather than lamenting the inadequacy of words, Yoori quietly attends to the brief silences that connect one word to another. As in the age of silence, her paintings blur the boundaries between the landscapes of life and those of language. Words that slip through the gaps between clearly held words, wavering phrases and sentences, are barely held together on the surface of her paintings.

For this reason, the exhibition 《Bind Loosely & Loosen Tightly》 is filled with ambiguous scenes. Yoori is interested not in fixed texts or words already spoken, but in things that never remain intact and continue to flow: the time that passes even now from one letter to the next, memories that grow increasingly faint and indistinct, and the relationships between this and that, between you and me. To describe these through painting is to engage with the condensed language unique to the visual medium. A gathering of signifiers detached from their signifieds and left adrift forms its own symbolic system and nuances within the image. In <To a Death Passing By>, divided into four panels, objects and landscapes lose both their relations to one another and their necessary ties to real-world referents, suspending the production of meaning.

The titles of the paintings return us once again to language, reminding us of the gap between text and image. Words associated with “weight” appear repeatedly: “the weight of certain events,” “light consolation,” or “heavy words.” Regardless of such modifiers, Yoori’s paintings somehow seem consistently weighty. They appear to take upon themselves what language has been unable to carry. Yet the loose connection between image and text always leaves openings through which things may enter or escape. As a symbolic motif visualizing such openings, “peripheral vision”² becomes a metaphor for the artist’s attentiveness to what lies at the margins (<With Peripheral Vision>). Forms that have slipped out of focus and become indistinct emerge at the forefront of the canvas, acquiring a new status and a new way of being seen.

Meanwhile, the book—language endowed with material form—may be the object closest to Yoori’s paintings. Through bundles of paper called books, our words have carried stories from the age of silence to the present. But where do the stories that could not be contained in books, the words left unwritten, go? Yoori’s paintings, which resemble books, begin with this question. The pages she casually opens extend into one long image, like a sentence without a period. In the painting sharing its title with the exhibition, objects come to resemble not the content of language—the text itself—but the sound of words spoken aloud. Within waves of varying tones, individual objects imbued with the luminosity of night arrive at <Bind Loosely & Loosen Tightly>. This fragile connection will continue to smolder quietly within the tiny crevices of things permeated by the light of night, in the unnameably silent spaces between silences.

There is something curiously similar between silence, understood as “a state of quiet without words,” and night, understood as “the darkness after sunset.” Both are temporary states and durations that pass beyond the realms of speech and daylight. Yoori’s paintings seek the time of silence and night because they cherish what is fleeting rather than eternal. The lives of the objects within her paintings will likely outlast our own time. For some reason, this fact offers a certain kind of consolation.

Amid the quiet order of things, there are paintings made without driving away silence or the night—paintings that somehow continue to speak, even without words or light.

¹ Nicole Krauss, The History of Love, translated by Min Eun-young (Munhakdongne, 2020), p.111.

² Peripheral vision refers to the visual capacity of the outer field of view. Though less acute and less sensitive to color than central vision, it is especially responsive to dim light and movement. (Standard Korean Language Dictionary).
