Monthly Art February 2024 [UP-AND-COMING ARTISTS]



Kim So-jung (Reporter, Monthly Art)

<A Poem Sent to Beings Uncalled by Language>


We have all had such an experience. A commonplace word suddenly becomes strangely unfamiliar, and in that moment we witness the object it denotes—usually left unnoticed in the ordinariness of everyday life—becoming “simulated” with the overwhelming presence of an elephant in the room. Language, as a social contract, is marked by all kinds of norms: standard rules, prescribed exceptions, and categories of usage. Every social act through which human beings name what is visible, imagine what is not, establish systems of thought, define phenomena, and communicate with others has originated from—and evolved through—the material of language.

Here, however, are ambiguous beings that do not belong to such a solid world. Yoori boldly summons onto the canvas those things that evade institutionalized language: forms beyond naming, abstract thoughts, and elusive emotions that play hide-and-seek between reality and presence. Made up of fragments of everyday life, these entities gather in <The Crossing of Imperfections> (2023) to seek their own worlds, compose the grotesque imagery of <A Loose Poem> (2023), or quietly endure the passage of <Time of Feathers and Fallen Leaves> (2023). With particular affection for those situated beyond the boundaries of language, the artist draws viewers into what she calls “the gaps in language.”

Having attended art middle school and art high school before enrolling in a fine arts program in Korea, Yoori had long resolved to live as a painter. Yet she experienced severe confusion between her work and her sense of self. Viewing painting as a means of emotional release, she made works that she described as acts of “vomiting things out,” before suddenly fleeing to Germany, almost as if escaping, where she devoted herself to studying language for a year. There, stimulated by a new environment, she was able to continue making art in her own way. More importantly, using a foreign language led her to become attentive to the (ir)rational relationship between language and reality. This awareness extended from her own experience of discovering how language shapes the speaker’s identity to the sociocultural questions surrounding objects that can only be designated by a particular language.

Yet despite her desire to reveal the hidden underside of language, Yoori’s work is far from declarative. The subjects that, from the outset, are “nothing more than gestures”¹ still seem unable to acquire proper names on the canvas, though they appear remarkably unconcerned by their ambiguous condition. Taking the forms of objects, bodies, or something in between, these figures are even dignified, as if proclaiming that the role of language has long been overestimated. <In the Furthest Sky from the Sky> (2023) presents six scenes that resemble stages in the transformation between humans and spiritual beings. The wooden-log-like human forms that fill each panel assume various poses as though situated within specific circumstances, yet they lay bare their shapeless existence free from temporal progression or causal relationships.

By constructing his canvases with ineffable subjects and uniquely heavy colors, the artist draws viewers into a state of immersion born of confusion. At the same time, Yoori offers clues through the titles of her works. Titles such as Sentences Made with Nonexistent Words or Objects in Between reveal her intention to give form to a nonverbal world. Among them, <A Loose Poem> most explicitly invites viewers to read the painting with an awareness of rhythm and the breathing spaces between lines. Like poetry, which conveys atmosphere and context rather than relying on the logical conjunction of words and sentences, the viewer interprets the visual language that the artist has varied and orchestrated through imagination and association.

Yoori believes that even if language permeates the entire world, multiplying infinitely and surviving across history, time, and generations, there will always remain blind spots forever beyond its reach—mysterious domains untouched by words. And so, within the dwelling she has created, where one can momentarily step outside the world imitated by language, we are free to enjoy the detours of unfamiliar words with greater ease.

¹ Kim Chun-su’s poem Flower concerns the object (or human being) that acquires existential status only when its name is called and its existence recognized.
