Arts & Culture Column for Segye Ilbo


For the Time After the End — Tenderly Calling Back Vanished Beings

Shin Lisa (Art Historian | Head of Planning Team, Hakgojae)



Black lines and masses rise from deep blue pools and flowing streams. Visual artist Yoori (b. 1994) has continued to create works that draw up unnamed emotions and mental images lingering within the mind, unable to evaporate. Her solo exhibition 《Shapeless Loop》 (11/19–12/20), currently held at Hakgojae, begins from a deeply personal narrative. After confronting the deaths of loved ones three times in a single year, the artist came to contemplate existence and reality through the concrete themes of life and death. In particular, the experience of receiving birthday wishes at a funeral hall — her birthday having become the anniversary of her grandfather’s death — led her to realize that birth and death are not separate events, much like the candles at a funeral and those atop a birthday cake resemble one another. This exhibition emerges precisely from that gaze directed toward the boundary between endings and beginnings, presence and absence.

<At the Place Where Things Have Ended>

The exhibition consists of around fifty works spanning painting, sculpture, and installation, most of them rendered in black, white, and at times deep blue tones. What Yoori holds onto here are the small and subtle remnants left behind by beings that can no longer be seen. Rather than concealing or beautifying them, she chooses to reveal them honestly.

In the painting <Shapeless Loop>, which shares its title with the exhibition, the figure of a deceased cat appears alongside a red thread tied around its leg in anticipation of a future reunion. In sculptures made of resin and wood, that same thread reemerges with immediate material presence. These works do not rely on memory or recollection, but begin from the lingering presence of things that remain even after disappearance. Black stains deeply absorbed into the surface, matte textures drained of moisture, and violently sweeping lines remain like the afterglow of eternity left behind after turbulent emotions have passed.

In <Sorrow Formed from What Remains>, beads taken from the artist’s maternal grandmother’s necklace are sealed in resin, embodying the wish that vanished beings might continue to remain somewhere, in some form. The tiny beads suspended within transparent masses transform into crystallizations of eternity, shaped into objects for “the time after death.” The artist’s hesitation over whether to preserve the relics intact or transform them into artworks was ultimately overcome by a desire for transcendence toward another dimension. Her words, “I had to do something,” feel less like resignation than a determination to move forward through grief and mourning.

<A Moving World>

Yoori reveals the links between presence and absence, life and death, not through abstract concepts but through concrete objects, scenes, and tactile experiences. Endless black loops, black threads forming volumes and ribbons as they rise toward the sky, and white rings resembling wings appear throughout the works.

In <Endlessly Rising and Constantly Emptying>, the structure of connection and circulation takes the form of a fountain. Its movement — surging upward, falling, and filling again — visualizes the immense flow of life in which presence and absence intersect. It is also the movement of the mind itself: the experience of being filled and emptied within the great wheel of existence. Resembling a human face with two eyes, the fountain appears to pour out emotions unreservedly while maintaining an air of detachment, as though it already knows that what fills will eventually empty, and what is empty will inevitably be filled again.

The belief that endings are not truly endings, and the fascination with transparent loops of connection, are also metaphorized through the relationship between the moon and the sea. Though physically too distant ever to touch, the moon’s gravity continuously pulls the sea, keeping it in perpetual motion. In <The Pull Between Moon and Sea>, dark blue waves and faint moonlight blur into one another, erasing their boundaries. The artist places these two eternally separated entities together within a house-like space. Like two beings far apart yet powerfully connected, she captures relationships that continue after separation and invisible forces that nonetheless operate with intensity.

<Fragments Born by Stepping Beyond the End>

In <The Long-Tailed Book>, reflections on connection and the will toward transformation emerge through the artist’s working process itself. Originally folding a seven-meter-long painting like an accordion, Yoori witnessed existing scenes accidentally linking together and generating new combinations. Images jutting out like unreadable sentences gave rise to other languages and meanings, leading the artist to separate these scenes into independent small paintings and expand them into a series titled <The Long-Tailed Book: A Detached Sentence 1-14>. She then miniaturized and reconnected those paintings once more, giving birth to a form resembling a single “tail.”

The act of folding the original work like a book is less an act of destruction than one of exploring new possibilities. Though the fragmented images lose their original function, the artist turns that rupture into the starting point of another beginning. Instead of preserving what remains — the original painting — intact, she fragments and reconstructs it, allowing the weight of “what remains” to speak anew. Thus, the works born “tail after tail” survive by stepping beyond the end, reborn as new beings — somewhat grotesque, yet gleaming black like living creatures.

<A Poem for What Remains>

In Yoori’s world, where what remains is gazed upon and allowed to regain light, nothing is meaningless and no ending is truly final. Emotions once sunk heavily like grief and longing ascend lightly toward another dimension upon white wings and transparent loops of eternity.

Although this exhibition begins with loss and mourning, it clearly contains a will to move beyond them and a yearning for eternity. There is an earnest hope that beings no longer visible might somehow continue their existence, and a desire to remain eternally connected to loved ones beyond physical limitations. Though profoundly personal, the reason we empathize with Yoori’s art may be that it ultimately touches upon the universal human fear of loss and the shared effort to endure and move beyond it.

Within Yoori’s art — born from life and returning once more to life — we confront fear and sorrow honestly while simultaneously sensing the possibility of transcendence.

To gaze upon the remaining fragments and quietly build poetry for the time after loss. In the deep stillness where sorrow settles and truth rises to the surface, on the final day of emotion, Yoori tenderly calls forth the nameless feelings and vanished beings left behind.
