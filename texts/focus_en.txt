Art in Culture, January 2025 [FOCUS]

Review of 《Golden Arrow》 (TINC, 2024)

Nam Woong


<Young Art Shoots for the Stars>

The exhibition 《Golden Arrow》 likens the moment a painter makes a brushstroke to the act of shooting an arrow into the darkness. In this metaphor, the shooting of the arrow becomes the act of creation; the arrow that ascends into the sky and becomes a star represents the artist of an era; and the light that offers direction within the darkness becomes noble art itself.

Art may have begun when someone pressed a fingertip into the earth and left a mark. Such a gesture—both a conscious act and an impulse to leave behind something that has departed—is inevitably bound to time. The act of holding onto what has passed, refusing to forget it, becomes a ritual that calls forth inspiration. Alongside time, one leaves traces of those who have departed, the wounds they left behind, gestures destined to disappear into darkness, and the trajectories of the sun, moon, and stars that existed before one's birth and will continue to exist after one's death.

Yet what remains can never be whole; it can never become the very time in which its subject once existed. What fills the space left vacant by time are records and acts of creation—arrows launched into a dark sky. For these arrows, aimed against the flow of time, to continue their flight, they must not only propose the future but somehow live it in advance.

One of the most striking features of the exhibition space is the series of plinths extending along both sides of a central passageway. Paintings rest upon sloped surfaces as though an arrow had cut a path through darkness. Despite the exhibition’s central motif, however, the star that the arrow supposedly becomes is nowhere to be found. Instead, only Sanoh Lee’s <Arrow> hangs in one corner of the gallery, its hollow shell remaining as testimony to the arrow’s passage.

The exhibition text establishes a vertical hierarchy, using the golden arrow shot into the heavens and transformed into a star as a metaphor for the artwork. Not every arrow launched skyward becomes a star, it suggests, thereby emphasizing the uniqueness and value of the artwork. Yet it may be more accurate to say that the exhibition visualizes the inspiration and impulses left behind by those who remember the significance of such an arrow, rather than celebrating a singular golden arrow that achieves artistic transcendence and autonomy.

Touches and Breaths Shared Between One Another

The paintings arranged on either side of the gallery appear like traces left by an arrow’s passage, or as creative outcomes that follow in its wake. While the golden arrow is shot upward toward the sky, the exhibition corridor runs parallel to the ground. Rather than presenting the autonomy of a sublime artwork shining like a distant star, the exhibition seems to reveal a struggle to navigate darkness through small lights. It evokes those who continue to live upon the earth, imagining the trajectory of an arrow that may once have passed overhead, groping through darkness and bringing forth residual gestures from the margins of light.

The tactile materiality of Yoori’s paintings leads into Sanoh Lee’s works, where optical flatness and amorphous movement unfold lightly across the surface as one approaches the stage-like area of the gallery. These are followed by Jinhyung Lee’s paintings, in which pigments accumulate as stains while context and recognizable form are gradually erased. The exhibition path subtly blurs distinctions between tactile and visual hierarchies, eventually arriving at Sanoh Lee’s three-dimensional sculpture.

Even this sculpture appears as a translucent object through which light passes. Rather than asserting the grandeur of sculptural form, it stands like the empty shell of something that has already passed, silently bearing witness. In staging a site of absence, the sculpture ultimately dissolves the hierarchical scenario implied earlier.

If so, one sentence from the exhibition text requires reconsideration. The phrase, “rewriting the art that leaves being from nothing so that it leaves being from being,” might instead be read as “the art that leaves behind as being that which has become nothing.” We create forms in longing for a dream, yet never fully reach it. Even so, such attempts reveal that their apparent futility is itself driven by desire, and that this desire continues to connect one moment of time to another.

From this perspective, what we call art may be understood as a residue that bears witness to the trajectory of the arrow and, in doing so, constructs the constellation known as the Golden Arrow.

The essence of art, symbolized by the golden arrow, shines brilliantly in the heavens. Yet most people live their lives upon the earth, building places to dwell. An arrow that travels not through the sky but across the ground may remain not as a star, but as a longing to become one.

It survives through those who remember the Golden Arrow as a legend; through those who move their bodies in places where the arrow once passed and darkness has since returned; through those who leave traces behind and, in doing so, confirm one another’s touch and breath.
