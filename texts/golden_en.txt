《Golden Arrow》

Sung-geun Kim (Curator, Director of Rainbowcube)



In the beginning, all light gathered, and a world of pure white opened.

An artist once said that standing before a pristine white canvas, before lifting the brush, fills them with fear. Why should an artist feel afraid when faced with the boundless freedom of creation—a space untouched by rules or restraint? As viewers, we have all encountered that white world before. We remember our childhood, drawing on sheets of paper with crayons and watercolor paints. There were moments when we hesitated, uncertain of what to draw, yet it was there that many of us first experienced the joy of making something that had never existed before. Perhaps, for some, that was where the dream of becoming an artist first began. So why, in that same place where freedom still exists, does the artist hesitate to lift the brush? After enduring years of repetition in pursuit of becoming an artist, the canvas should have become familiar, even comforting. What was it that the artist came to see there? What unseen presence had become so immense that it rendered even the lightest brush impossibly heavy?Within that immaculate, empty world dwells a weight invisible to those who merely stand before it as spectators.



Then words, writing, and colors came into being upon the earth, and together they rose into the void, becoming the black universe.

The first artist. Tens of thousands of years ago, someone pressed a single finger into the sand. In that solitary mark, they sensed something strangely familiar, and with that gesture, humanity's history of art quietly began. No history of art records this first artist. Yet from that forgotten beginning, generations of makers have built the vast history of artistic creation. Layer upon layer, over countless years, new works have accumulated upon those that came before them. Every generation has added another stratum, extending an uninterrupted continuum that has never retreated, insisting only on moving forward. The living narrative of art continues to expand its boundaries. Within it reside innumerable paintings, sculptures, images, moving pictures, words, texts, and ideas. None of these have disappeared. Embedded deep within the geological layers of what we call history, they continue to support the ground beneath our feet.



There was darkness. Yet we cannot see this immense accumulation of human art, layered as thickly as the earth itself, enough to darken the whole world. This is both our instinct—to notice only the small stars scattered across the night sky—and our limitation, for we tend to follow only the stories preserved through records. Without the countless grains of sand and layers of soil filling the earth beneath us, there would be nowhere for our feet to stand. We would never even be able to lift our gaze toward the sky, drifting forever through the vast emptiness of the universe. And without the darkness that fills the cosmos, no shining star could ever have been discovered.




Then light came into being.

Not everything launched toward the heavens becomes a star. This is both an undeniable reality and an inevitable one. If every creator throughout history had been remembered as an artist, the very meaning of the word artist would have gradually dissolved until it disappeared altogether. To be remembered as an artist of one's time is both the aspiration of the artist and the force that drives creation. Without the small stars scattered across the night sky, we would lose our sense of direction. Without the precious works of art left upon the earth—like treasures unearthed from beneath its surface—the world we inhabit today would be filled only with disposable commodities and abandoned waste. To leave behind a work that comes to define an era is both the aspiration of the artist and the force that drives creation.


Art is often described as the creation of something from nothing. In reality, however, every new work is built upon the countless "somethings" that came before it. For every artist, this is an inescapable fate: to create not only works of art, but also another form of creation that emerges beyond the creations left by those who came before, as well as by fellow artists living in the same era. To choose the life of an artist is therefore to stand upon the ever-expanding frontier of humanity's artistic history, while directing one's practice beyond its existing boundaries. Yet even those who faithfully pursue this path will not all be remembered in the history of art. This is a difficult truth. Once an artist comes to recognize it, lifting the brush is no longer easy. (And yet, not everyone needs to shoulder such a monumental destiny. There is equal wisdom in lifting the brush as lightly as a feather and simply embracing the joy of making.) Add to this the realities of exhibitions and criticism, the cost of materials and everyday living, the endless voices of others, and the constant uncertainty surrounding one's artistic direction, and the brush grows heavier still. Unlike physical training, where the stronger the body becomes the easier it is to lift weight, art works in the opposite direction. As time passes, even this increasingly weightless brush becomes ever more difficult to raise.
(But again, no one is obliged to bear the full weight of art. There is wisdom, too, in simply finding joy in the act of creation.)





One who stands firmly upon the earth and reaches toward the sky.

One who overcomes the endless darkness.

One who advances in the hope of becoming a star.

The brush is lifted,

its tip begins to shine.

Your hands are gold,

and that gold is pure.





Standing before a blank white canvas, the artist must have seen not emptiness, but the accumulated presence of all the art humanity has ever made. Confronted with the immense destiny contained within that seeming void, it is only natural that the artist hesitates before lifting the brush. And yet, they lift it. They sharpen its tip and press it into the canvas, tracing the path of a new creation. Before us now stands the noble result of those who chose to plant their feet upon the earth while reaching into the vast emptiness beyond. Our role is simply to watch them in quiet silence as they move forward despite their fear, and to wait together for the moment when the tip of the brush begins to shine.







*


The arrow left the earth and soared toward the sky.

Driven by the desire to become a star, it continued its flight into the vast, dark expanse of the void without ever coming to rest. Then, at some unknown moment, a faint light began to glow at its tip. As viewers, we could only witness this silent gesture with quiet attention.

Golden Arrow envisions the artist as one who extends the ever-expanding boundaries of art, building new expressions upon the foundations laid by generations before. The exhibition invites us to look not only at the works themselves, but also at the artist's way of living—a continual movement toward the pursuit of new creation.

Like an arrow flying beyond the known horizon into the endless black universe, the artist advances with courage toward what lies outside established boundaries. Our place, as viewers, is simply to watch those who continue forward in quiet silence, patiently awaiting the moment when that arrow begins to shine as a star.

Perhaps this is, after all, one way of approaching both the artwork and its maker, allowing us to arrive at what we call art itself.

Much like those who once received, in silence and reverence, the words and lives of beings believed to have attained the sublime, we too are invited to encounter these works with quiet attentiveness.
