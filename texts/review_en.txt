《Shapeless Loop》 : Exhibition Review



Kim Haeseo (Writer)

<Here Is Where We Meet>



   I once had a white dog. His tail was long and sleek, and whenever he stood in the sunlight, his dark eyes took on a sharp, cola-brown hue. A Jindo mix with alert, pricked ears. We called him Heunggu.


   Long ago, I let him go. Yet the moment I step into my mother’s hometown in Oeseo-myeon, Suncheon—a village thick with bamboo—I still catch myself whispering “Shh—” inside, trying to quiet the dog who comes alive again and barks. Yes. Heunggu was my maternal grandfather’s dog. Technically, I never had him. To him, I was perhaps just a girl he saw on occasion—someone who carried a faint, vaguely unpleasant scent of the city. A girl, standing among adults, her face indifferent, sometimes drifting close to stroke his head and the bridge of his nose, then turning away—only to wrinkle her brow and flinch in disgust if he so much as licked her palm. That incomprehensible girl.

   My grandfather died only a month after his cancer diagnosis. Shortly after, Heunggu was gone without a trace. By my grandmother's unilateral decision, the dog was handed over to someone else. It may have been the dog her husband had cherished that unsettled her as deeply as the loss of the man itself. Heunggu was quite old, my mother and her siblings talking about his future—‘What about burying him next to Father?’ ‘Wouldn’t it be better to scatter him in the mountains?’—and I watched, with something like pity, to see what a dog looks like when it has lost its master, and then,  he was simply gone. Everyone was appalled, when it came to light. But before my grandmother’s cold face, no one dared say a word. And after that, no one showed any worry or longing for him.

   Neither did I. Their heartlessness startled me, but Heunggu had been my grandfather’s dog and that made him my grandmother’s matter. I had seen him only a few times at holidays or family gatherings. It … should not have been a loss that mattered.

   So I couldn’t understand it - every time his ghost barked from nowhere.

  A few weeks before the dog left, the adults began unclipping his leash more often, letting him wander the yard. His happy paws made a tododododo sound across the cement and didn’t stop. I heard it later; while my grandfather was ill, when everyone packed up and rushed to the hospital as if evacuating, Heunggu had survived by rationing the feed my grandfather had left, more than usual. When one of my uncles came by two weeks later, it wasn’t empty. He had even  left some behind. After that, another long wait. The person who had said he’d be back soon never came through the gate again.

   As my grandfather’s belongings were being sorted, I listened half-waiting for Heunggu’s name to surface in the adults’ talk. My grandfather loved him. But that is as far as I know.

   The day I heard that my grandmother had led only my father—her son-in-law—out to the field, turning her own children away, and burned a few of her husband's favorite clothes and small belongings, collapsing as she called out the names of her husband and her eldest son who had died in an accident—I thought of her face at the funeral, blank, not a sound, and I stopped wondering about Heunggu.

   Now I quietly track Heunggu. Or rather—his traces. As time passes, his wholeness blurs and only pieces remain—the way I picture my grandfather only in parts: his thick hands, his gold-toothed smile, his straw hat. The soft tap of Heunggu's claws across the yard has become indistinguishable from the sound of bamboo clashing beyond the wall. Whenever the village dogs set off barking at a strange car, I think of him. But Heunggu would stop the moment he knew we were "us." And so within all that noise, what I felt stayed mine alone.

*
   In December 2025, I saw the exhibition 《Shapeless Loop》 by artist Yoori. For reasons I cannot quite name, this exhibition set something adrift in me—guilt, emptiness, and a longing I had no right to feel. It may have begun with the animal form in the work <A Far and Black Stain>. The four-legged shape—impossible to tell whether foal or dog—touched something inside me: my dog, a mark that would not fade. My white dog may no longer be white. He will have become something formless, something that cannot be likened to anything. The connection between us has come undone across the Styx, yet the dog settles into the life that continues as an abstract layer. And each time that layer falls across my dimension, we meet. Sometimes as the sound of footsteps. Sometimes as a cry. Sometimes as a connection that can only be explained as "mistaken" or "imagined."
   The work <The Potential of Loss and Eternal Yearning> is perhaps the most perfect "book" to embody that fantastical contact. I believe I turned and read the pages of this book, shaped from transparent resin and wooden blocks. Inside the wooden book, painted over in muted pigment, a beast is dismembered within the transparent resin—unraveled by red thread and cloth. Like a heart dissolving in water, vivid yet coming apart into line and plane. No longer oxidized by air, fixed forever in a vacuum: what had once been a body. If I felt that this transparent book was asking to be understood—that there are signals which can only be sent through silence—would that be too much?
   Between me and the white dog, between me and what was once the white dog, between me and what is no longer the white dog—if a single thread still runs, I think I now have the courage to tap it gently. Even if nothing stirs in return.
   The images in <The Long-Tailed Book> and <The Long-Tailed Book: A Detached Sentence(1–14)>, encountered afterward, showed a narrative given without consent between life and death—yet came to me as something confused, reaching out then folding back, as if grasping at a will to connect a murky past to an uncertain future. Like a collection of punctuation marks made by someone who must explain an entire life just to speak a single word—exclamation points and question marks, commas and periods all thrown together. Is this what the moments have been like for artist Yoori, from 2023 when the artist book series began until now? And is this truly how time moves in the world where we, the surviving, remain?
   Soaked in a giddy fear, as if I had witnessed some terrible secret, I looked once more at <Shapeless Loop>, hanging at the entrance. The two flowers—life and death—are so alike they might as well be twins. Both look fresh; both look somehow wilted. They seem to grow in different directions, yet in the end they are bound by a single ribbon. The "shapeless loop" is a force exactly as powerful—and as fragile—as that small ribbon.
*
   More than ten years have passed since my grandfather died. Now, instead of a white dog, yellow cats live in the old house in Suncheon. My grandmother, who said she never wanted to grow attached to anything again, has at least allowed them the yard and the back garden. She throws them scraps now and then. Thanks to this, the yellow cat family has been settled there for three generations, living well. They were wary at first, but with each generation they grow a little more used to human hands. My mother and her siblings send bags of cat food whenever they can—so my grandmother, whether she likes it or not, will have to make peace with the sound of meowing at her door.
   I still visit occasionally, as a stranger from a distant city, and give some affection to the cats. And I think of my dog. Looking into their amber eyes, I see cola-colored eyes. White lashes. A long, warm tongue. Black paw pads. A tail wagging at the sight of me.

*The title is taken from John Berger's Here Is Where We Meet (Youlhwadang).
