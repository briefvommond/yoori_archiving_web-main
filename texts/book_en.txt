Monthly Art, August 2025 [Artist’s Book Special Feature]

Yoori (Artist)


<Composing the Unreadable>


To trace the origins of my artist’s book practice, I must first uncover a somewhat crooked desire from my childhood. From a very young age, I was fascinated by stacks of paper bound into the form of a book. In particular, I was obsessed with preserving notebooks I loved in a perfectly pristine state. I was always eager to acquire new notebooks, and I would inevitably fill them with my drawings and writings. Yet if I felt that the traces of my hand had diminished the aesthetic quality of those pages in even the slightest way, I could not tolerate it. I would immediately tear out the offending page. Of course, the torn page would then damage the integrity of the notebook itself, and the poor thing would eventually be abandoned, forgotten in some corner, where it would remain forever asleep. For this reason, I was often scolded by my parents throughout my school years.

When I was in kindergarten, I once coveted a friend’s diary so much that I secretly took it home. Being caught by my parents and dragged to the front of a police station for a stern lesson has since become a family story that never fails to provoke laughter. Thankfully, that memorable experience cured me of stealing, but my desire for book-shaped objects never truly disappeared. Even now, as an adult, I continue to collect art books, make handmade diaries and exhibition guestbooks, and surround myself with notebooks and books. I have even opened bookbinding classes of my own, teaching binding techniques both in my studio and elsewhere. Eventually, books became part of my artistic practice as well. It seems fair to say that my affection for these accumulations of individual sheets has continued uninterrupted throughout my life.

What is it, then, about books that captivates me so deeply? (Here, “book” refers broadly to both conventional publications and bound notebooks.) When we think of the most common form of a book, we imagine countless thin sheets of paper layered together into a surprisingly solid mass. My fascination with artist’s books began with an interest in this particular sense of volume that emerges through accumulation. I am endlessly drawn to the visual qualities of books: the strength and form created when fragile sheets gather into a dense block; the methods of binding that transform separate pages into a unified whole; and the outer skin that envelops them.

Beyond this formal attraction, the quality of *connection*—the way pages are physically bound together and contents conceptually linked—became the decisive reason I embraced the artist’s book as part of my artistic practice as a painter. When developing paintings, I often imagine multiple images simultaneously or envision sequences of images unfolding one after another. I needed a way to gather them together, and I was interested in exploring meanings that could only emerge when those images existed collectively. The book naturally presented itself as the ideal medium. It allows narratives that cannot be contained within a single pictorial surface to continue beyond the frame while simultaneously binding them within a shared structure. In this sense, the artist’s book functions as a medium capable of sustaining continuity while creating a coherent whole.

What, then, does the artist’s book mean to me? While the distinction is not always clear-cut, I understand an *art book* as a broad category encompassing books that deal with artistic content, whereas an *artist’s book* is an artwork in which the artist’s intervention and attitude are central to every stage of the process, from production and distribution to circulation and sale. Accordingly, my artist’s book practice does not simply adopt the conventional form or concept of a book. Rather, I value the creation of alternative forms and concepts through artistic interpretation. I pursue the making of books through hand-binding, and I prioritize their value as exhibition works.

The ways in which one interprets and employs the form and symbolism of the book become defining characteristics of an artist’s book practice. Within books, countless ideas and narratives are inscribed in various forms of writing, traversing the surfaces of paper. The pages themselves accumulate layer by layer, occupying physical space and creating volume. Vast bodies of human knowledge gather through the language of text and ultimately become the solid form of a book.

By paradoxically employing this essential symbolism of the book as a repository of language, I deliberately exclude decipherable written language from my artist’s books and instead introduce images that resist clear definition as a form of visual language. In doing so, I create books that cannot be read, using them as a means of exploring the relationship—and the gap—between language and visual imagery.

When I was very young, I once cut and gathered various kinds of paper, bound them together in my own way, and presented them to my mother as a birthday gift. Could that perhaps be considered my first artist’s book? My earliest works consciously identified as *handcrafted artist’s books* consisted of collections of printed images bound together by hand. These books were primarily distributed and sold through independent bookstores. I would produce editions of around twenty copies entirely by hand, exhibiting and selling them in cafés and bookstores, or introducing them directly to audiences at art book fairs and art markets.

At that time, I was deeply immersed in the medium of the book itself. I tended to confine the possibilities of the book by imagining specific modes of distribution and display, and I often regarded it as existing within a context separate from my painting practice. Artist’s books shown in bookstores and book fairs were inevitably treated as publications, and in that capacity I encountered various limitations and frustrations. Through this process, I began to ask how works that resemble books might exist not merely within the category of “books,” but as independent artworks within a broader artistic context.

Gradually, my artist’s books became liberated from the structures of publishing, printing, and distribution. They began to exist as artworks in their own right. Rather than seeing books resting quietly on display tables, I wanted to see them moving and playing within exhibition spaces. Bringing this medium into the gallery allowed me to explore its possibilities alongside painting. I became increasingly interested not only in the form and concept of the book itself, but also in the ways it could be installed, positioned, and placed in dialogue with other works within an exhibition.

One of the key works marking this transition is *<Dummy>*, an artist’s book project initiated in 2020 and continued as an ongoing series. It played a crucial role in the early stages of developing the artist’s book as an autonomous artistic practice. The word “dummy” can refer both to a large accumulated mass of objects and to a “dummy book,” a preliminary bound model. *<Dummy>* imitates the form of a book, yet it is an unreadable book composed of oil paintings on oil paper that have been stacked and bound together. By hand-binding original paintings into book form, the work eliminates reproducibility and transforms individual paintings into a new kind of aggregate. It becomes a book that can be read but cannot truly be read; a work that encourages viewers to physically turn its pages and encounter the paintings directly; a work situated somewhere between painting and sculpture. Through this project, I began to observe these conditions and discover clues for future developments in my practice.

The exhibition *<Double Bind>*, held at Pokettales in Euljiro in 2023, was another pivotal moment in my journey with artist’s books—important enough that I described it as an “artist’s book project.” It served as a reflection on the various book-based works I had produced up to that point while also revealing my thoughts on future directions. Beginning once again with the fundamental question, “What is a book?”, I examined the medium from the ground up, exploring one of its defining characteristics—connectivity—by alternately reinforcing and loosening it.

Works in the exhibition included *<Heavy Words>*, a book in which images are deeply engraved into wood as though heavy words had sunk beneath the surface, rather than lightly printed onto thin sheets of paper; *<Little Coffin Book>*, a work in which viewers themselves create the binding by placing sculptural pages one by one into a coffin-shaped box; and *<Dog eared page>*, a book whose spine has been detached and whose pages have been dispersed and displayed separately. Through these works, I sought to move beyond fixed ideas of what a book should be and to blur the boundaries of the medium itself.

Since then, I have continued to expand my understanding of the book through experiments with materials, forms, and methods of installation. My practice has gradually evolved toward an expanded concept of the book.

Whenever I sew images together with needle and thread, or bind them with glue into the form of a book, I feel as though I am connecting images to a medium, linking my inner world to the external world, and ultimately creating connections between myself and others—or even between myself and the world at large. Beginning with the physical act of binding, the process invites me to imagine invisible connections that extend far beyond the material object itself. This practice allows my senses to reach in multiple directions, filling spaces that painting alone cannot occupy and functioning in a complementary relationship with my painting practice.

What pages might I unfold, tear away, or turn through in the future? And how might I weave them together?
