Shapeless Loop for Our Annyeong(安寧)

Han Joo-ok (Independent Curator) 

At times, memory becomes a trigger for glimpsing what lies ahead. When a scene from the past unexpectedly cuts through the present, it recalls the patterns that have carried the world forward while simultaneously becoming an omen of what is yet to come. It is the moment when different temporal lines fold into one. Every trajectory—in nature and in human life—eventually converges into the present. Perhaps this is why connection has long been represented as an infinite loop, a trajectory with neither origin nor terminus. For a long time, the artist Yoori has made this sense of connection the central subject of her practice. She traces sensations that have slipped into the past and observes the emotions that accumulate in the space left by absence, remaining attentive to their potential to return in another form. Within this practice, in Shapeless Loop, the artist gathers moments when recurring feelings of loss collide with ordinary scenes in daily life. These are sensations perceived before language and signals that appear at the threshold of logic. She composes them into a transparent loop.

In her studio, she recounted several recent experiences. At her grandfather’s funeral, an unexpected moment broke through the solemnity of the gathering: a birthday song suddenly filled the room. The candles flickered, their light crossing with incense and tears. The scene stayed with her, as life and death surged into awareness all at once. Rather than reading these experiences through metaphysical concepts such as reincarnation, the artist turns her attention to sensing the currents that emerge in the unfolding of events. Encounters with what is invisible and resistant to representation have led her to consider perception as a temporal configuration. This is perhaps what could be called temporality. Her practice lies in allowing perception to shift, transform, and take on new forms. Her works become sites where past and future fold into the present, where multiple layers of time coexist.

Her working process makes this approach even more evident. By repeatedly layering, erasing, and connecting what lies inside and outside the surface, she resists fixing an image as a final outcome. Each captured scene acts as a point of mediation, calling forth another and gaining momentum to shift into the next. Her experimentation with materials expands in this continual process. What stands out is that each work functions as a narrative unit. That is, the work operates as a kind of sequence. It gathers, arranges, and revisits scenes, forming a temporal loop and establishing the artist’s distinctive structural language. This is especially evident in the works she has long referred to as her Artist’s Books.
In this exhibition, the artist presents The Book of Weighty Words #1–19 (2023–2025), a series of Artist’s Books she has been producing since 2023. Perceived through touch and surface, the Artist’s Books become a metaphor not only for what cannot be verbalized but also for her act of connecting them. The artist has noted “the power of a book formed by multiple sheets of paper gathering into a single mass, and the physical and visual stimulus that emerges from the binding process.” Within her practice, the book becomes a physical embodiment of connection and accumulation. Scenes unfold as if pages are being turned, and each act of reading generates another link that extends to the next. Her recent works, Sorrow Formed from What Remains (2025) and The Potential of Loss and Eternal Yearning (2025), introduce a layer of transparency to this trajectory. Acrylic ink, silk fabric, and objects that once remained in the artist’s hands are sealed within resin, solidifying into the form of a book. At the same time, the premise that one can look through them remains. Once again, past and future flow into the present.

And so, the question lingers. What remains on the surface? The faint light left by a dying candle, a ribbon that loosens and ties again, running water, and fragments of a luminous wing. These images emerge from the time that lingers within the artist, remnants of moments that stayed briefly and then disappeared. The meanings they carry resist being fully grasped and remain beneath the surface of her consciousness. For this reason, she chooses not to define them. Instead, she receives the flow as it comes, and lets it pass again. One is reminded of the moment when the path once believed to be straight began to soften into a curve. And doesn’t that curve open onto another path? The loop has long been represented in this way. Here, the idea materializes through two works installed on the right side of the central exhibition space. They are The Long-Tailed Book: A Detached Sentence (1-14, 2025) and The Long-Tailed Book (2023–2025). The first work took shape as a seven-meter installation. Through repeated binding and unfolding, accidental divisions emerged, splitting scenes apart and becoming catalysts for a new sense of connection. From this process, The Long-Tailed Book: A Detached Sentence came into being. Images that once formed a continuous narrative now diverge within the book. They become independent scenes, yet still maintain continuity, connecting like a loop.


One day, the absence of someone—a trace slipped out of time and space—returns with a quiet sting. She does not hide the pull of what has slipped away. As she gathers what remains, one fragment at a time, a form gradually takes shape. It becomes Sorrow Formed from What Remains. At the entrance, Shapeless Loop (2025) condenses birth, death, and the absence of what is cherished into a single event. A deep blue-black tone settles across the surface like slow water along a canal at night. Realizing that the (im)material beliefs that once held our daily lives together have disappeared brings a pain that resists being dispelled. Here, mourning gathers and anchors itself to the space, reminding us that the most fundamental condition of connection begins in absence.


Translated by Han Joo-ok
